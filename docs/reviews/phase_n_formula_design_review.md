## Current Formula Interpretation

### Goldilocks

Current formula:

- rewards growth_momentum
- penalizes positive inflation_pressure
- rewards credit_liquidity
- rewards policy_stance near zero
- rewards positive yield_curve

Economic meaning:

Goldilocks should mean decent growth, contained inflation, benign credit/liquidity, neutral policy pressure, and a non-threatening curve.

Current assessment:

The formula is directionally coherent. It correctly loses on 2026-05-01 because growth is weak and inflation pressure is high. No immediate Phase O change is needed.

### Reflation

Current formula:

- rewards growth_momentum
- rewards inflation_pressure
- rewards credit_liquidity
- rewards policy_stance
- rewards positive yield_curve

Economic meaning:

Reflation should mean growth and liquidity are improving while inflation is rising from low/normal levels, not an inflation-stress regime.

Current assessment:

This formula is too additive around inflation. On 2026-05-01, reflation gets a large boost from high inflation and easy credit, even though growth is weak. It stays close to stagflation because it can treat high inflation as good without asking whether growth is also healthy.

Risk:

If reflation continues to reward high inflation linearly, it may stay too competitive during stagflation-like periods.

### Stagflation

Current formula:

- rewards weak growth
- rewards high inflation
- rewards tight policy
- penalizes easy credit/liquidity
- penalizes positive yield_curve

Economic meaning:

Stagflation should require high inflation and weak growth together. Ideally, it should not be generated by high inflation alone or weak growth alone.

Current assessment:

The direction is coherent, but the additive form is too blunt. The 2026-05-01 stagflation result is economically explainable because inflation pressure is high and growth is weak. However, stagflation remains close to tightening because both regimes receive the same large inflation contribution and similar credit/curve treatment.

Risk:

If stagflation is additive, it can overlap with tightening whenever inflation is high, even if the growth distinction should be decisive.

### Recession

Current formula:

- rewards weak growth
- penalizes positive inflation pressure
- rewards credit stress
- rewards accommodative/easy policy as a recession response
- rewards yield-curve weakness

Economic meaning:

Recession should mean broad demand weakness, ideally confirmed by credit stress and/or curve weakness. It should not win only because growth is soft when credit and the curve are benign.

Current assessment:

The formula is mostly coherent, but recession and stagflation still correlate meaningfully because both like weak growth and risk stress. On 2026-05-01, recession is correctly held down by high inflation, easy credit, and positive yield curve. This suggests the current formula behaves acceptably in the latest month, but historical overlap still deserves testing.

Risk:

Over-sharpening recession around credit/curve can make it too late or too noisy, as the Phase L stronger credit/curve variant increased switches and lowered confidence.

### Tightening

Current formula:

- rewards positive growth only
- rewards high inflation
- rewards credit stress
- rewards tight policy
- rewards yield-curve weakness

Economic meaning:

Tightening should mean inflation pressure plus restrictive policy while growth has not yet collapsed. It is a late-cycle policy-pressure regime, not a recession or stagflation regime.

Current assessment:

This formula is the main overlap problem with stagflation. On 2026-05-01, tightening receives the same inflation boost as stagflation, the same credit penalty, and the same yield-curve penalty. Weak growth simply contributes zero instead of actively penalizing tightening. That allows tightening to remain close when it probably should be pushed down more strongly by weak growth.

Risk:

If tightening only ignores weak growth instead of penalizing it, tightening can remain too competitive during stagflation/recession-like periods.

## Dimension Role Review

### growth_momentum

Should distinguish:

- positive growth: goldilocks, reflation, tightening
- negative growth: stagflation, recession

Current issue:

Tightening uses positive_only, so weak growth does not hurt it. This is likely too forgiving.

### inflation_pressure

Should distinguish:

- high inflation: stagflation, tightening
- moderate rising inflation with growth: reflation
- low/contained inflation: goldilocks
- high inflation should generally suppress recession unless growth/credit/curve weakness is severe

Current issue:

Reflation and tightening both get strong linear support from high inflation. Reflation likely needs a cap or near-zero/moderate-inflation reward, while tightening should pair inflation with restrictive policy.

### policy_stance

Current interpretation:

Dimension polarity makes higher/tighter policy stance negative at the dimension layer. A negative policy_stance score therefore reads as easier/less restrictive conditions, while a positive score reads as tighter/restrictive conditions.

Current issue:

The naming can be confusing because regime polarities invert this score differently. Tightening and stagflation both currently use negative polarity on policy_stance, so both can benefit from the same policy state. Phase O should test whether policy should distinguish these regimes more explicitly.

Possible design:

- tightening should benefit from restrictive policy/tightening pressure
- stagflation should benefit from restrictive policy only when inflation is high and growth is weak, or from policy being insufficiently supportive despite weak growth
- recession should benefit from easier policy only as a response signal, but should not rely on policy alone

### credit_liquidity

Should distinguish:

- benign/easy credit: goldilocks, reflation
- stressed/tight credit: recession, stagflation, tightening

Current issue:

Credit/liquidity is behaving coherently in the latest output: easy credit opposes stagflation and recession. The Phase L stronger recession credit/curve variant was worse, so credit should be handled carefully. Do not simply increase credit weight aggressively.

### yield_curve

Should distinguish:

- positive curve: goldilocks, reflation
- inverted/weak curve: recession, tightening, possibly stagflation

Current issue:

Yield curve is currently a small weight across regimes. It helps in the right direction, but it may not be strong enough to separate recession from stagflation or tightening from stagflation.

Risk:

Increasing curve weight too much may overfit yield-curve regimes and make signals late or noisy.

## Known Overlap Problems

### Stagflation vs Tightening

Core problem:

Both regimes reward high inflation and treat credit/curve similarly. Tightening does not actively penalize weak growth; it only ignores it through positive_only.

Current date example:

- Both receive +0.417 from inflation_pressure.
- Both receive -0.108 from credit_liquidity.
- Both receive -0.028 from yield_curve.
- Stagflation receives +0.162 from weak growth.
- Tightening receives 0.000 from weak growth.

This creates separation, but not enough.

Design implication:

Tightening should probably require growth resilience and restrictive policy together. Weak growth should actively penalize tightening.

### Recession vs Stagflation

Core problem:

Both regimes like weak growth and adverse risk/curve conditions. The key distinction should be inflation: recession should be more plausible when inflation pressure is low/falling, while stagflation requires inflation pressure high.

Current date example:

Recession is held down correctly because inflation is high and credit/curve are benign. That is good. Historical correlation remains meaningful, so Phase O should test sharper inflation gating rather than simply increasing credit/curve weights.

Design implication:

Recession should require weak growth plus at least one confirming stress signal, with high inflation acting as a stronger penalty unless credit/curve stress is severe.

## Proposed Phase O Formula Experiments

### Experiment O1: Tightening Requires Growth Resilience

Design:

- Keep inflation_pressure positive.
- Keep policy_stance as the primary differentiator.
- Change growth_momentum from positive_only to a function that rewards positive growth and penalizes negative growth.

Possible config idea:

```yaml
tightening:
  growth_momentum:
    polarity: positive
    weight: 0.20
  inflation_pressure:
    polarity: positive
    weight: 0.30
  policy_stance:
    polarity: negative
    weight: 0.30
```

Expected effect:

Tightening should fall behind stagflation when growth is weak.

Risk:

If growth softens before policy turns, tightening may disappear too early.

### Experiment O2: Stagflation Inflation x Weak Growth Interaction

Design:

Stagflation should require high inflation and weak growth together. Keep additive terms, but add a true interaction and reduce standalone inflation/growth weights.

Possible formula idea:

```yaml
stagflation:
  additive:
    inflation_pressure: positive
    growth_momentum: negative
  interactions:
    inflation_x_weak_growth:
      inflation_pressure: positive_only
      growth_momentum: negative_only
```

Expected effect:

Stagflation should become less likely when only inflation or only weak growth is present.

Risk:

The Phase L simple interaction test did not improve current separation, so the interaction design needs a different weighting scheme or nonlinear form.

### Experiment O3: Reflation Inflation Cap

Design:

Reflation should reward moderate rising inflation, not extreme inflation pressure.

Possible formula idea:

```yaml
reflation:
  inflation_pressure:
    polarity: reward_near_zero
    or: capped_positive
```

Expected effect:

Reflation should stop clustering near stagflation when inflation pressure is high and growth is weak.

Risk:

If inflation is measured as a z-score, reward_near_zero may punish legitimate early-cycle reflation too much.

### Experiment O4: Recession Weak Growth Plus Confirmation

Design:

Recession should require weak growth and confirmation from either credit stress or yield-curve weakness.

Possible formula idea:

```yaml
recession:
  interactions:
    weak_growth_x_credit_stress:
      growth_momentum: negative_only
      credit_liquidity: negative_only
    weak_growth_x_curve_weakness:
      growth_momentum: negative_only
      yield_curve: negative_only
```

Expected effect:

Recession should not win too easily when growth is weak but credit and curve are benign.

Risk:

Could make recession detection too late if credit stress lags growth.

### Experiment O5: Policy Stance Sign Audit

Design:

Run variants that explicitly test whether policy_stance should:

- support tightening when restrictive
- support stagflation only conditionally
- support recession when easing appears as response

Expected effect:

Clarify whether policy_stance is adding separation or reinforcing overlap.

Risk:

Policy has interpretation ambiguity: high rates can mean restriction, while falling rates can mean recession response.

## Recommendation For Phase O

Phase O should be formula experiments only, not production formula replacement.

Recommended variants:

1. Tightening growth-resilience variant.
2. Stagflation interaction variant with reduced standalone inflation/growth weights.
3. Reflation inflation-cap variant.
4. Recession weak-growth-plus-confirmation variant.
5. Policy stance sign audit variants.

Primary success metrics:

- lower stagflation vs tightening raw-score correlation
- lower recession vs stagflation raw-score correlation
- current confidence improves without over-concentrating probabilities
- historical average/median confidence improves
- low-confidence periods decline
- regime switch count does not rise materially
- dominant regime distribution remains economically plausible

Do not expand sources yet. The next useful work is a better formula experiment design, not more data.
